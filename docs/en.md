# Deep Software Engineering

— Toward a Software Engineering Where All Actors Participate in the Conversation —

[日本語版](index.html)

## Introduction

This document is a statement on Deep Software Engineering.

We believe that the problem software engineering has grappled with for half a century deserves to be reexamined in depth, now that we have language models as a new kind of tool. This document sets out that belief.

## The Problem Software Engineering Discovered

From its very beginning, software engineering has consistently addressed one essential problem.

Multiple **actors** are involved in software, and they share no common language.

- **Machines** — hardware, the physical substrate of computation
- **Algorithms** — software, the procedures of computation
- **People** — developers, users, and other stakeholders
- **The grounded** — the physical, social, embodied, and living reality to which symbolic language refers

Enabling conversation among these actors: this is the problem at the heart of software engineering, and everything ultimately converges on this single point.

### Classical Software Engineering

Classical software engineering, which developed from the 1970s through the 2000s, correctly identified this problem. But the means it adopted to solve it — voluminous documentation, rigid processes, artificially defined common languages — ended up suppressing the very conversations it sought to facilitate.

The enormous cost of producing and verifying documents led to rigid processes. Language use was imposed beyond the cognitive limits of people. Poorly grounded metrics were adopted, driving misguided optimization. The methods classical software engineering employed ultimately brought about its own stagnation.

### Post-Agile Software Engineering

Post-agile software engineering, from the mid-1990s to the present, sought to overcome the rigidity of the classical approach. It emphasized feedback, responsiveness, distribution, and concurrency, and expanded both the kinds of actors and the kinds of languages used among them.

We use the word "language" here in a broad sense. Pattern languages, graphical models, frameworks, formal languages and type systems, domain-specific languages, scripting languages, methodologies, and practical notational forms such as code, cards, and words — all of these are "languages" that mediate conversation among actors.

This polyphonic development was welcome. But the creativity, individual diversity, and critical thinking that early Agile and Lean emphasized were gradually replaced by mechanical iteration, uncritical adoption of standardized frameworks, and service to the giant platforms of global capitalism.

And, in a trajectory parallel to classical software engineering's stagnation, the post-agile movement appears to be heading toward the same impasse.

### The Actor Left Behind

Classical and post-agile software engineering share one critical shortcoming.

**The grounded has always been excluded from the conversation.**

In classical software engineering, reality was an object to be transcribed into requirements documents. In post-agile software engineering, reality is an object to be fragmentarily described in user stories and models. In both cases, the grounded is "that which is described," an object of representation — never an actor participating in the conversation.

Yet the grounded is not a passive object. The grounded —

- Imposes constraints (physical laws, social norms, bodily limits)
- Returns feedback (behavior that defies expectations, unpredictable change)
- Changes on its own (the grounded itself is altered by software's intervention)

The grounded is at once the subject of conversation and a participant in it — or at least, it ought to be.

## A New Condition

A major change is now arriving. The rapid spread of language models.

We deliberately use the term "language model" rather than vague phrases like "generative AI." We also omit "large." We expect that scale will cease to be essential in the future.

Regardless of whether language models are good or bad, their impact cannot be ignored. And language models open unprecedented possibilities for the problem software engineering has wrestled with for half a century — conversation among actors.

### Language Models Are Tools

We do not position language models as a fifth actor, nor as a centralized translation apparatus.

**Language models are tools that each actor possesses.**

This framing carries important implications.

- **Distributed**: Rather than a single translator at the center, each actor converses with other actors through its own language model
- **Subordinate to the actor**: Tools are used in accordance with the actor's intent and context. Language models do not dominate the conversation; actors lead it
- **Different for each actor**: The language model a person uses need not be the same as the one an algorithm uses

### What Becomes Possible

We believe this tool makes the following possible.

- Documents can be generated and modified at low cost, with a reasonable degree of consistency
- Document verification becomes feasible to some extent, through cross-checking between language models and (semi-)formal descriptions
- Translation between natural language and the languages used by non-human actors becomes feasible to some extent
- The languages used among actors can shift from compromised lingua francas toward languages optimized for specific domains and actors
- And above all, **the possibility opens for the grounded to gain a voice in the conversation** — sensor data, physical constraints, social feedback, and more can take a form amenable to dialogue through language models

The conditions are now emerging to address, by different means, the problem that classical software engineering discovered.

## What Deep Software Engineering Aims For

We believe it is necessary to envision **a software engineering in which all actors, including the grounded, can participate in the conversation**, and to explore concrete methods and frameworks for achieving this.

Specifically, we believe the following questions should be pursued.

1. **Redesigning languages**: How can the languages used among actors (pattern languages, models, frameworks, type systems, domain-specific languages, etc.) be redesigned in the presence of language models?
2. **Participation of the grounded**: What technical and methodological mechanisms can enable the grounded to participate as an actor in the conversation?
3. **Rebuilding shared understanding**: To what extent can the shared understanding among actors — which classical software engineering attempted to achieve imperfectly through documentation — be realized through the combined use of language models and (semi-)formal methods?
4. **Questioning language itself**: What is the expressive power of language? Is language used creatively, or conventionally and as cliche? Is meaning sufficiently shared and properly grounded? Are language and action, language and thought, appropriately connected?

These questions are not confined to software engineering. But we begin from the concrete domain of software engineering.

## About This Statement

This document is a statement written at a stage where there are neither results nor plans. It is not a promise of outcomes, but a sharing of concerns.

If what we have stated resonates with you — or if you have objections or questions — we welcome the conversation.
